Research Workshop — Three Years Later

Innovation Beyond Safety

While competitors rush to manufacture basic safety elevators, you've taken a different approach. You understand that safety alone isn't enough—passenger elevators need to be comfortable, reliable, and efficient.

You've developed several innovations: hydraulic cushioning to smooth the stop at each floor, automatic floor-leveling mechanisms, enclosed cabs with proper ventilation, and an improved braking system that engages gradually rather than suddenly.

Your prototype is significantly more expensive to build than standard models, but the ride quality is incomparably better. Early testers describe it as "riding on a cloud" compared to the jarring stops of conventional elevators.

A wealthy hotel owner in Manhattan has expressed interest. He's building a luxury hotel and wants the finest elevator available. He's willing to pay premium prices for premium quality.

However, a major department store chain has also approached you. They want to install elevators in five locations but need a more affordable option. The volume order would establish your company financially but requires simplifying your advanced design.

You must choose: perfect the luxury market or adapt for mass adoption.

Innovation Dilemma
Many inventors face this tension between perfection and accessibility. The best technology doesn't always win—sometimes "good enough" technology that reaches more people has greater overall impact. Henry Ford's assembly line made cars affordable for average Americans, not just the wealthy.
Which market will you pursue?
