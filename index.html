A Dangerous Problem

You are Elisha Graves Otis, a 41-year-old mechanic working at a bedstead factory. For years, you've built machines and solved problems that others thought impossible.

Last month, tragedy struck at the Collins Textile Mill downtown. A freight platform carrying three workers dropped four stories when its hemp rope snapped. Two men died on impact. The third survived but will never walk again.

The incident has haunted you. Every factory, every warehouse, every construction site uses these rope hoists. They fail regularly. People die regularly. And as buildings grow taller to meet demand, the problem only worsens.

You've been sketching a solution late at night: a spring-loaded safety brake that engages automatically if the lifting rope breaks. It's simple in theory, but untested.

Your employer wants you focused on bed frames. Your wife worries about the family budget. But you can't ignore the problem.

Historical Context
In the 1850s, buildings rarely exceeded five stories because stair-climbing was impractical for tenants and workers. Freight hoists existed but used hemp or manila rope that degraded over time and could snap without warning. The lack of safe vertical transportation was a major constraint on urban development and industrial growth.
