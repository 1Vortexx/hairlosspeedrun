Otis Manufacturing — Five Years Later

Building an Industry

Your successful demonstration opened doors. With investor backing, you've established Otis Steam Elevator Company. Your factory employs forty workers and produces safety elevators for buildings across New York and beyond.

The demand is extraordinary. Architects are designing buildings seven, eight, even nine stories tall—heights that would have been impractical just a decade ago. Your elevators make vertical living and working space economically viable for the first time in human history.

But rapid growth brings new challenges. To meet demand, you've had to train new workers quickly. Quality control is inconsistent. Some installations have experienced mechanical problems—not catastrophic failures, but concerning nonetheless.

A prestigious developer wants to install your elevators in a new luxury apartment building on Fifth Avenue. It will be the tallest residential building in the city at ten stories. The contract is worth more than all your previous sales combined.

However, your head engineer has concerns. He believes the current cable system, while adequate for freight and shorter buildings, needs reinforcement for taller passenger applications. The modifications would delay the project by three months and increase costs by 30%.

The developer is impatient. He's heard rumors that competitors are developing their own safety elevators. If you can't deliver on schedule, he'll look elsewhere.

The Challenge of Scaling Innovation
Many inventors struggle with the transition from prototype to production. Manufacturing at scale introduces new problems: maintaining quality, training workers, managing supply chains, and balancing innovation with reliability. The skills required for invention differ from those needed for business management.
