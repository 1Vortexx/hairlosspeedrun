New Workshop — One Year Later

A Second Chance

The accident cost you clients, money, and sleep. But it also gave you clarity. You've rebuilt your workshop with a single purpose: perfecting the safety elevator.

You've spent your savings developing a comprehensive safety system. The automatic brake engages if rope tension drops. A speed governor prevents dangerous acceleration. Multiple redundant mechanisms ensure that no single point of failure can cause catastrophe.

More importantly, you've visited the injured worker and his family. You've listened to their story. You've helped them financially, not from legal obligation but from moral responsibility. That conversation changed you.

Now, you've received an invitation to demonstrate your safety elevator at an upcoming industrial exposition. Several investors will attend. This is your opportunity to prove that safety and commercial success aren't mutually exclusive.

But you face a choice in how to demonstrate it. You could stage a dramatic presentation—cutting the rope while standing on the platform—which would certainly capture attention. Or you could provide detailed technical documentation and controlled tests, which would be more thorough but less memorable.

The Power of Redemption
Many significant innovations came from inventors who experienced failure first. Thomas Edison famously said he didn't fail 10,000 times while developing the light bulb—he found 10,000 ways that didn't work. The willingness to learn from mistakes, take responsibility, and persist despite setbacks often separates successful inventors from forgotten ones.
