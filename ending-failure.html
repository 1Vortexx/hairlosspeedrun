The Elevator Inventor: An Interactive History

ENDING: The Cost of Compromise

When Profit Comes Before Safety

You defend your work vigorously. The warehouse owner requested a basic hoist. You delivered exactly that—a functional freight lift at the agreed-upon price. The rope failure wasn't your fault; it was normal wear and tear. The industry standard accepts these risks.

Your arguments fall on deaf ears. The injured worker's family files a lawsuit. Several newspapers run stories about the accident, questioning whether the rush to industrialize has made workplace safety an afterthought.

Other potential clients distance themselves from you. The investor who expressed interest in your safety brake design withdraws his offer, not wanting to be associated with controversy.

Within a year, you're forced to close your workshop. You return to working for others, building conventional machinery. Your safety brake prototype sits unused in a crate in your basement.

In 1854, a man named Elisha Otis—not you—demonstrates a similar safety elevator at the Crystal Palace Exhibition. His dramatic presentation, cutting the hoisting rope while standing on the platform, captures public imagination. Within a decade, his company revolutionizes urban architecture.

You watch from the sidelines, knowing you had the same idea years earlier but lacked the conviction to pursue it fully.

What This Ending Teaches Us

This path illustrates several critical lessons about engineering, innovation, and responsibility:

  • Safety requires commitment: Building "industry standard" equipment when you know it's unsafe makes you complicit in foreseeable accidents.
  • Short-term thinking has long-term costs: Prioritizing immediate profit over innovation can mean missing transformational opportunities.
  • Reputation matters: In engineering and business, your name is attached to your work. One accident can define your career.
  • Timing and conviction: Having an idea isn't enough—you must pursue it with determination, even when it's difficult.
