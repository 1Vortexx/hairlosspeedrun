Your Workshop — Six Months Later

Learning From Failure

The accident haunts you, but it also crystallizes your purpose. You've returned to your original safety brake concept with renewed determination. This time, you're not cutting corners.

You've spent months refining the mechanism. The new design uses multiple redundant safety features: a primary automatic brake that engages when rope tension drops, a secondary manual brake, and a speed governor that prevents free-fall even before the rope breaks.

You've tested it hundreds of times with progressively heavier loads. You've deliberately stressed every component to failure to understand weak points. You've documented everything meticulously.

A regional manufacturing association is hosting an industrial exposition next month. They've invited inventors to demonstrate new technologies. This is your opportunity to show what you've learned—to prove that safety doesn't have to be sacrificed for cost or convenience.

But the demonstration will be public. If something goes wrong, your reputation—and possibly someone's life—will be at stake.

Engineering Lesson
Many significant innovations in safety engineering came from analyzing failures. The practice of "failure mode analysis"—systematically identifying how things can fail and designing preventive measures—became standard in aerospace and mechanical engineering. Learning from mistakes, rather than hiding them, drives technological progress.
