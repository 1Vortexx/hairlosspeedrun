Riverside Factory — One Week Later

Compromises and Consequences

You've completed the installation on schedule. The factory owner insisted on a basic rope-and-pulley system. "I don't need fancy safety mechanisms," he said. "I need it to move goods now."

You reluctantly agreed, though you included reinforced rope and a simple brake lever. The $200 payment covers three months of expenses—your family is grateful.

Two weeks later, disaster strikes. A worker overloaded the platform with barrel staves, exceeding the rated capacity by nearly double. The rope held, but when he engaged the brake too suddenly, the platform lurched violently. He lost his balance and fell three stories onto the concrete floor below.

He survived but suffered severe spinal injuries. He'll never work again.

The factory owner blames the worker for overloading. The worker's family blames you for not building adequate safeguards. Local newspapers report the accident, and your name is mentioned as the builder.

That night, you pull out your sketches of the automatic safety brake. You realize you compromised your principles for money, and someone paid the price.

The Engineering Ethics Dilemma
In the mid-1800s, workplace safety regulations were virtually nonexistent. Engineers and builders faced constant pressure to deliver cheap, fast solutions. Accidents were common and typically blamed on worker carelessness rather than design flaws. The concept of "engineering ethics" as a formal discipline wouldn't emerge until the early 1900s.
