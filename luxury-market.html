ENDING: The Artisan's Path

Perfection for the Few

New York City — 1865

Your elevators are works of art. Each installation is custom-designed, engineered to perfection, and finished with meticulous attention to detail. Wealthy hotels, exclusive clubs, and luxury apartment buildings feature your creations.

Passengers describe riding your elevators as transcendent experiences—smooth as silk, quiet as a whisper, stopping with such precision that they barely notice the transition. Your hydraulic cushioning system and vibration dampening innovations are years ahead of competitors.

You've built seventeen elevators in ten years. Each one costs three times what competitors charge. Each one is flawless.

Meanwhile, other manufacturers have installed thousands of elevators. They're not as refined as yours, but they're safe and affordable. Buildings are rising across every major American city. The urban skyline is transforming.

You've perfected the technology but reached only a tiny fraction of the market. Your innovations remain in luxury properties. Working-class people still climb stairs. Most factory workers will never experience the smooth ride you've perfected—they can't afford buildings with your elevators.

Your work is technically superior. Your reputation among the elite is unmatched. But you wonder sometimes: did you optimize for the wrong variable? Is perfection that reaches seventeen buildings more valuable than good-enough technology that reaches thousands?

What This Path Teaches Us

This ending reveals an important tension in innovation:

  • Excellence vs. Access: Perfecting technology for the few may be less impactful than making "good enough" technology available to many
  • Market choices matter: Who you design for shapes the technology's eventual impact on society
  • Artisanship has value: There's genuine worth in pursuing perfection, even if it doesn't maximize total impact
  • Multiple paths to contribution: Setting the highest standard can inspire and guide others, even if you don't achieve mass adoption
  • Trade-offs are inevitable: You cannot simultaneously optimize for perfection and accessibility—you must choose
